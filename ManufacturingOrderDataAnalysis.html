Operational Dynamics and Strategic Resource Planning in Katana MRP: A Comprehensive Analysis of the 'Make' Module

1. Introduction: The Strategic Architecture of Modern Manufacturing Resource Planning

In the contemporary landscape of manufacturing, the transition from static, spreadsheet-based tracking to dynamic, real-time Material Requirements Planning (MRP) is a critical inflection point for scalability. The 'Make' module within Katana MRP represents a paradigm shift in how production activities are conceptualized and executed. Unlike legacy Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems that often segregate planning from execution via batch processing and rigid modules, the Katana 'Make' interface functions as a unified command center. It bridges the strategic imperative of inventory control with the tactical necessities of shop-floor execution.

The core philosophy underpinning this module is the concept of "Live Inventory." In traditional manufacturing management, inventory data is often historical—reflecting what was on the shelf at the time of the last manual count or batch update. Katana’s architecture, however, posits that inventory is a fluid state, constantly altered by the interplay of incoming procurement, outgoing sales, and the transformative process of manufacturing itself.1 The 'Make' screen is the visual manifestation of this flux, providing a real-time ledger of future commitments (Manufacturing Orders) and their immediate impact on current resources.

This report offers an exhaustive analysis of the 'Make' module, dissecting the logic of its primary components: the Schedule tab and the Tasks tab. It explores the algorithmic underpinnings of "Rank" and "Ingredients Availability," the financial implications of its costing methodologies, and the operational workflows that govern the lifecycle of a Manufacturing Order (MO). By understanding the nuances of these mechanisms, operational leaders can leverage the system not merely as a tracking tool, but as a strategic engine for optimizing throughput, minimizing working capital, and ensuring on-time delivery.

2. The Manufacturing Order (MO): Entity Architecture and Classification

The fundamental unit of data and operational control within the 'Make' module is the Manufacturing Order (MO). To the uninitiated, an MO might appear simply as a digital instruction to produce goods. However, within the Katana ecosystem, the MO is a complex data container that serves as the nexus for inventory reservation, cost accumulation, and production scheduling. Understanding the disparate behaviors of different MO types is prerequisite to mastering the broader system.

2.1. The Dichotomy of Production: Make-to-Order vs. Make-to-Stock

Katana distinguishes between two primary categories of Manufacturing Orders, each governed by distinct workflows and logical constraints: Make-to-Order (MTO) and Make-to-Stock (MTS). This distinction is not merely semantic; it fundamentally alters how the system prioritizes work and calculates availability.3

2.1.1. Make-to-Order (MTO) Logic

Make-to-Order MOs are the direct manufacturing response to recognized demand. They are triggered specifically by a Sales Order (SO), creating a hard, digital umbilical cord between the customer’s request and the production floor.

  • Permanent Linkage: A defining characteristic of an MTO MO is its permanent link to the underlying Sales Order line item.3 This relationship is immutable; the system treats the MO and the specific SO as tightly coupled entities. This prevents the "orphan order" phenomenon common in less integrated systems, where a sales order is cancelled, but the production order continues unnoticed, resulting in unwanted inventory. In Katana, if a Sales Order is modified (e.g., quantity changes) or cancelled, this information can ripple through to the linked MO, ensuring alignment between commercial commitments and operational activity.4
  • Attribute Locking: Because of this linkage, certain attributes of an MTO MO are locked to preserve data integrity. The product variant and the planned quantity cannot be edited directly on the MO card because they must mirror the customer's specific request on the SO.3 To change the quantity of an MTO MO, one must adjust the Sales Order itself, enforcing a workflow that respects the sales contract as the source of truth.
  • Priority Synchronization: Perhaps the most critical operational feature of the MTO logic is priority synchronization. The priority (or "Rank") of an MTO MO in the manufacturing queue is automatically synced with the priority of its parent Sales Order.3 If a sales manager, responding to a VIP client's urgency, drags a Sales Order to the #1 position in the 'Sell' screen, the associated MO automatically jumps to a corresponding high-priority position in the 'Make' screen’s Schedule tab. This automated alignment eliminates the friction between sales and production departments, ensuring that the factory floor’s priorities are always an accurate reflection of commercial urgency.

2.1.2. Make-to-Stock (MTS) Logic

In contrast, Make-to-Stock MOs are speculative or replenishment-driven. They are created to restore inventory levels to a safety stock target or to prepare for anticipated seasonal demand, rather than to fulfill a specific, existing customer order.

  • Independence: MTS MOs function as autonomous entities. They are not linked to a specific Sales Order, although the inventory they produce will eventually be claimed by future sales orders. This independence allows for bulk optimization; a planner might aggregate demand from ten small sales orders into a single, large MTS run to maximize machine efficiency and minimize setup times.3
  • Flexibility: Users have full administrative control over the product selection and quantity on an MTS MO. These orders can be created manually via the global "+" button, or generated in bulk via the Stock screen when inventory levels dip below defined Reorder Points.5
  • Decoupled Priority: Unlike MTOs, the priority of an MTS order in the Schedule tab is managed independently of the Sales screen. Adjusting the rank of an MTS order is a purely operational decision. However, this decision has downstream effects: while it doesn't rearrange sales orders, it does affect which sales orders can be fulfilled. A high-priority MTS order will claim raw materials first, potentially enabling the fulfillment of multiple sales orders once completed.3

2.2. The Anatomy of the MO Card

Regardless of its classification (MTO or MTS), every Manufacturing Order encapsulates three distinct data layers that drive the 'Make' module's functionality.

  1. General Information: This header data includes the MO number, creation date, production deadline, and the specific location (warehouse or factory) where production will occur. The location field is particularly vital for multi-site operations, as it determines which inventory pool the MO will draw from and replenish.
  2. Ingredients (Bill of Materials): This section is the engine of material planning. It lists all raw materials and subassemblies required to execute the order. Crucially, while it pulls initial standard data from the Product Recipe (BOM), it allows for "Actual" usage reporting.2 This flexibility is essential for handling real-world variances, such as material waste, scrap, or substitutions.
  3. Operations: This section details the procedural steps required to produce the item (e.g., Cutting, Assembly, Packaging, Quality Control). It lists the specific Resource (machine or workstation) and the Operator (human) assigned to each step, along with columns for planned versus actual time.6 This granularity transforms the MO from a simple "to-do" item into a detailed project plan.

The interaction between these layers is dynamic. A change in the General Information (e.g., changing the location) recalculates the Ingredients Availability based on the stock at the new site. A change in the Operations (e.g., reassigning a task) updates the resource schedule in the Tasks tab. This interconnectedness ensures that the MO serves as a single source of truth for the entire production process.

3. The Schedule Tab: The Logic of Rank and Dynamic Reservation

The Schedule tab is the primary interface for production planners and serves as the "Command Center" for the 'Make' module. Its grid layout displays critical columns including Rank, Manufacturing Order #, Product, Production Status, Ingredients Availability, and Deadlines.7 While all columns provide value, the operational heart of this screen—and arguably the most distinct feature of Katana’s logic—is the Rank column.

3.1. The Rank Logic: A Dynamic Waterfall Reservation Model

In many traditional ERP and MRP systems, inventory allocation follows a First-In-First-Out (FIFO) logic based on the order creation date, or it relies on "hard allocation," where a user must manually "tag" specific inventory batches to specific orders. These methods are often rigid; changing a priority in a hard-allocation system requires un-allocating stock from Order A to re-allocate it to Order B, a tedious and error-prone process.

Katana utilizes a dynamic, rank-based reservation system (often referred to as "Soft Allocation").8 The "Rank" acts as a priority queue that governs the distribution of limited resources. The logic follows a strict top-down "waterfall" algorithm:

  1. System Scan: The system continuously scans the list of open Manufacturing Orders, starting from Rank #1 and proceeding downwards to the last order.
  2. Reservation Check: For the MO at Rank #1, the system references the Product Recipe to identify required ingredients. It then queries the Stock database for available "In Stock" quantities of those ingredients.
  3. Allocation: If the ingredients are physically available, they are logically reserved or "soft-allocated" for Rank #1. The availability indicator for this order turns green (In Stock).
  4. Progression and Deficit: The system then moves to Rank #2. It checks availability minus what was just reserved for Rank #1. It proceeds down the list. Eventually, it may reach an order (e.g., Rank #20) where the physical stock is exhausted.
  5. The "Not Available" State: Even if the warehouse physically holds 100 units of "Steel Sheet," if MOs ranked #1 through #19 collectively require 100 units, MO #20 will display "Not Available" for its ingredients. To the planner, this signals that the stock is effectively gone, claimed by higher-priority work.2

3.1.1. The Operational Implications of Reprioritization

The power of this system lies in its agility. Drag-and-drop actions in the Schedule tab are transactional, not merely cosmetic. If a planner drags MO #20 up to the Rank #2 position, the system immediately recalculates the entire reservation waterfall.

  • Immediate Effect: MO #20 (now Rank #2) immediately claims the required materials, likely turning its availability status to "In Stock."
  • The Ripple Effect: Conversely, the orders that were previously ranked #2 through #19 are effectively "bumped" down. The materials they previously held claim to have been usurped by the new high-priority order. This may cause their availability indicators to flip from Green ("In Stock") to Red ("Not Available").8

This mechanism places immense power in the hands of the planner. It allows for instant responsiveness to changing business needs (e.g., a rush order from a key client) without the administrative burden of manual de-allocation. However, it also demands a disciplined approach; careless reordering can disrupt the material planning for the entire shop floor, inadvertently stalling long-running jobs by stripping them of their allocated materials.

3.2. Ingredients Availability Logic: The Traffic Light System

The Ingredients Availability column is a computed status that acts as a traffic light for the planner, aggregating the availability of every single line item in the MO's Bill of Materials. The status of the MO is determined by the worst-case status of its constituent ingredients—if ten ingredients are in stock but one is missing, the MO is flagged as constrained.2

3.2.2. The Inventory Intel Window

Clicking on the availability status triggers the Inventory Intel popup. This diagnostic tool reveals the calculations behind the simple Green/Yellow/Red status.2 It is essential for troubleshooting material shortages. The window displays:

  • In Stock: The total physical count currently in the warehouse.
  • Expected: The total inbound quantity from open POs or MOs.
  • Committed: The total quantity required by all open manufacturing and sales orders combined.
  • Reservation List: A crucial, prioritized list showing exactly which orders are reserving the ingredient. The current MO is highlighted in bold. This allows a user to see, for example, that "MO #105 is blocked because MO #102 is using the last engine block." This transparency allows the planner to make informed decisions—perhaps MO #102 is less urgent and can be deprioritized to release the engine block to MO #105.

3.3. Production Statuses: The Workflow Lifecycle

While Ingredient Availability is calculated by the system, the Production Status is a state variable updated by the user (or shop floor operator) to reflect the physical progress of the work. This status drives inventory accounting and financial ledger updates.9

3.3.1. Status Definitions and Inventory Impact

  • Not Started: The default state upon creation.
  • Inventory Impact: Ingredients are marked as Committed. The finished product is marked as Expected. No physical stock has moved.
  • Work in Progress (WIP): Production has commenced. This status is often triggered automatically when an operator presses "Start" in the Shop Floor App.
  • Inventory Impact: Ingredients generally remain Committed rather than being deducted immediately. This "back-flushing" approach keeps the stock visible until completion, though the financial value acts as Work-in-Progress. The finished product remains Expected.
  • Partially Complete: Used when a production run is long, and some units are finished and available for sale before the entire order is done.
  • Inventory Impact: The Finished Goods Stock increases by the specific quantity marked complete. The Ingredients Stock decreases (is consumed) based on a pro-rated calculation of the planned quantity. The remaining balance stays as Expected/Committed.9
  • Done: The order is fully executed.
  • Inventory Impact: Finished Goods Stock increases by the full quantity. Ingredients Stock decreases based on the actual reported usage (which may differ from the plan).
  • Financial Impact: Final costs are calculated. The value of the consumed ingredients and labor is transferred from the raw materials/WIP asset accounts to the finished goods asset account. The MO is effectively closed and moved to the "Done" history.9

3.3.2. The "Blocked" Status Anomaly

The Blocked status represents a unique exception state. It is used to signal a stoppage due to external factors (e.g., machine breakdown, quality hold, missing tooling).10

Crucially, setting an MO to "Blocked" does not release its inventory reservations.10 The system assumes the blockage is temporary and maintains the claim on materials to prevent them from being "stolen" by other orders.

  • Operational Risk: If a high-priority MO is Blocked for a significant duration (e.g., waiting weeks for a spare part), it acts as a "dam" in the waterfall logic. It continues to reserve materials that might be needed by active, lower-priority orders, potentially starving the rest of the production line.
  • Best Practice: If an order is Blocked for a long term, it should be manually de-prioritized (dragged to the bottom of the Schedule) to release its hold on materials. When the issue is resolved, it can be re-prioritized.

4. Material Requirements and Availability Dynamics

The "Make" module is inextricably linked to the "Buy" (Procurement) and "Stock" modules. The intelligence of the 'Make' screen relies on its ability to identify not just what is needed, but how to get it.

4.1. Resolution Workflows: The "Buy" and "Make" Decisions

When the Ingredients Availability indicator turns red ("Not Available"), the planner has two primary resolution paths directly from the MO or Ingredients view. These workflows are designed to minimize clicks and ensure data continuity.

4.1.1. The "Buy" Workflow for Raw Materials

If the missing ingredient is a purchased raw material:

  • Trigger: The user clicks the "Buy" button next to the missing ingredient in the MO's ingredient list.11
  • Process: This action opens a Purchase Order (PO) creation modal. The system is context-aware: it pre-fills the supplier (based on the default supplier set in the Item Card) and the required quantity to fulfill the deficit.
  • Consolidation: To optimize shipping costs, the user can choose to "Add other missing items from the same supplier." The system scans all other open MOs, identifies any other shortages from that specific vendor, and aggregates them into a single PO.12
  • Result: Once the PO is created, the Ingredient Availability status on the MO changes from "Not Available" to "Expected." The date displayed updates to the "Expected Arrival Date" of the new PO.

4.1.2. The "Make" Workflow for Subassemblies

If the missing ingredient is a subassembly (an intermediate product manufactured in-house):

  • Identification: The system identifies that the item is a manufactured good via its Product Recipe (it has a BOM of its own).13
  • Process: When creating the parent MO, if the subassembly is missing, Katana logic dictates that this demand must be met by production. The user can create a subordinate Manufacturing Order for the subassembly.
  • Recursive Availability: This creates a multi-level dependency. The parent MO (e.g., "Bicycle") cannot be "In Stock" until the child MO (e.g., "Wheel Assembly") is "Done." The parent MO's ingredient status changes to "Expected," linked to the Production Deadline of the child MO.
  • Automation: Katana supports features to automatically generate these subassembly MOs when the parent MO is created, ensuring that the full depth of the Bill of Materials is scheduled.13

4.2. Multilevel BOMs and Recursive Planning

Katana handles Multilevel Bills of Materials (BOMs) by treating subassemblies as standard inventory items that happen to have a "make" origin rather than a "buy" origin.

  • Visibility: In the Schedule tab, subassembly MOs appear as distinct rows, just like final product MOs. This flat-list view allows planners to see the total load on the shop floor, rather than hiding sub-manufacturing inside a "black box" parent order.
  • Synchronization: The deadline of the subassembly MO is naturally driven by the requirement date of the parent MO. Planners must ensure that subassemblies are ranked appropriately high in the schedule to ensure they are produced before the parent MO requires them.

4.3. Unit of Measure (UoM) Conversion

A frequent challenge in manufacturing is the discrepancy between how materials are bought and how they are used. A factory might buy steel in "sheets" but consume it in "square meters," or buy oil in "drums" but use it in "liters."

  • Mechanism: Katana addresses this via UoM conversion logic defined on the Item Card. The 'Make' screen respects these conversions.14
  • Application: When a planner clicks "Buy" for a missing ingredient tracked in "liters," the resulting Purchase Order can automatically convert the request into "drums" for the supplier, ensuring accurate ordering while maintaining precise usage tracking on the MO.

5. The Tasks Tab: Resource Management and Execution

While the Schedule tab manages the "What" (Products) and "When" (Deadlines), the Tasks tab manages the "How" (Operations) and the "Who" (Resources/Operators). This view shifts the unit of analysis from the Order to the Operation.6

5.1. Granularity: Decomposing the MO

The Tasks tab "explodes" the Manufacturing Orders into their constituent operational steps. If MO #101 requires three steps—"Cutting," "Welding," and "Painting"—the Tasks tab will display three distinct rows for MO #101.

  • Resource Leveling: This granular view is essential for identifying bottlenecks. A planner might see that while the "Schedule" looks clear, the "Tasks" view reveals that the "Welding Station" has 50 hours of work queued up for a 40-hour week.
  • Filtering: Users can filter the Tasks tab by Resource (e.g., show only tasks for "CNC Machine A") or by Operator (e.g., show only tasks assigned to "Jane Smith"). This allows for targeted capacity management.6

5.2. Resource vs. Operator Assignment

Katana makes a semantic and functional distinction between Resources and Operators, a nuance that is critical for accurate planning.

  • Resources: These are typically workstations, machines, or locations (e.g., "Assembly Table 1," "Oven B"). Resources are defined in the Product Recipe. When an MO is created, the system automatically assigns the operations to the default Resources.
  • Operators: These are the human users (employees) who perform the work. Operators are assigned to tasks in the Tasks tab.15
  • The Link: A Resource (e.g., "Welding Bay") can be staffed by different Operators. The planner's job in the Tasks tab is often to assign a specific Operator to a Resource-based task. This assignment is the trigger that pushes the task to the Shop Floor App. Without assigning an Operator, the task remains visible only in the back-office planner view and does not appear on any worker's digital device.15

5.3. Task Statuses and Workflow

Task statuses mirror the MO statuses but function at a micro-level, providing granular progress tracking.

  • Open: The task is planned and assigned but not yet started.
  • In Progress: The operator has clicked "Start" in the app. The timer is running.
  • Done: The operation is completed. The item logically moves to the next operation in the sequence.
  • Blocked: This status can be set if an operator encounters an issue at their specific station (e.g., tool breakage). Unlike blocking the whole MO, blocking a task is specific to that operation, though it naturally delays subsequent steps.16

6. The Shop Floor App: Bridging the Physical-Digital Divide

The 'Make' module extends beyond the browser-based dashboard via the Katana Shop Floor App. This mobile-friendly interface is designed for the operators on the factory floor, replacing paper travelers and manual time-sheets.17

6.1. The Operator's Perspective

When a planner assigns a task in the Tasks tab, it appears instantly on the assigned operator's device (tablet or smartphone).

  • Task Lists: Operators see a prioritized to-do list. They do not need to worry about the complex "Rank" logic of the main schedule; they simply execute the tasks in the order presented.18
  • Digital Interaction: The interface is simplified. Large buttons allow operators to "Start" and "Finish" tasks. This ease of use is crucial for adoption in industrial environments where computer literacy may vary or where gloves/safety gear make complex data entry difficult.

6.2. Real-Time Data Capture: Actuals vs. Planned

One of the most valuable features of the Shop Floor App is the capture of "Actual" data.

  • Time Tracking: When an operator hits "Start" and later "Finish," the app records the precise duration of the task. This replaces "guesstimated" labor times with empirical data.19
  • Material Consumption: The app allows operators to report the actual quantity of ingredients consumed. If a recipe calls for 1.0 meters of wood but the operator had to discard a damaged section and used 1.2 meters, this can be recorded immediately.
  • Feedback Loop: This data feeds directly back into the MO in the 'Make' module. Planners can see "Planned Time" vs. "Actual Time" side-by-side. This feedback loop is instrumental for refining costing models and identifying inefficiencies (e.g., if a specific task consistently takes 20% longer than planned, the standard cost and schedule should be updated).20

6.3. Limitations and Constraints

It is important to note certain constraints of the app to understand the workflow fully.

  • No "Block" Action: Interestingly, the Shop Floor App does not typically allow an operator to "Block" a task directly (e.g., stop it and flag it as broken). The standard actions are Start, Pause, and Finish.18 If a blockage occurs, the operator typically pauses the task and verbally communicates with the manager, who then updates the status in the main web interface.
  • Consecutive Enforcement: The app can be configured to enforce consecutive operations, preventing an operator at Step 2 (Assembly) from starting their work before Step 1 (Cutting) is marked as Done.21

7. Costing and Financial Logic: The Bottom Line

The 'Make' module is not just a logistics engine; it is the primary source of data for Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and inventory valuation. Inaccurate use of the 'Make' screen leads to inaccurate financial statements.

7.1. The Two Layers of Cost

Every Manufacturing Order tracks two parallel sets of cost data:

  1. Planned Cost: Calculated at the moment the MO is created.
  • Ingredients: (Planned Quantity from BOM) $\times$ (Current Average Cost in Stock).
  • Operations: (Estimated Time) $\times$ (Cost Per Hour of the Resource).
  • Utility: This provides a baseline for quoting and budgeting.
  1. Actual Cost: Calculated when the MO is marked "Done."
  • Ingredients: (Actual Quantity Consumed) $\times$ (Average Cost at moment of consumption).
  • Operations: (Actual Time Spent) $\times$ (Cost Per Hour).
  • Utility: This is the value that hits the balance sheet.

7.2. Moving Average Cost (MAC) Valuation

Katana utilizes a Moving Average Cost methodology for inventory valuation.22 This has profound implications for the 'Make' module.

  • Timing Matters: The cost of ingredients is locked in at the average cost at the time of usage. If a batch of raw material was purchased at a higher price due to a market spike, the MO executing during that period will reflect a higher production cost.
  • WIP Valuation: While an MO is "In Progress" (WIP), the value of the materials is technically in a transition state. Katana’s system keeps the value in the "Raw Materials" and "WIP" asset categories until completion. Upon the "Done" status, the aggregated value (Materials + Labor + Overhead) is transferred to the "Finished Goods" asset account.
  • Variance Analysis: By comparing the Planned vs. Actual costs on the MO card, managers can perform variance analysis. A consistent negative variance (Actuals > Planned) suggests that either the production team is inefficient (scrap/waste) or the BOM/Recipe underestimates the true requirements.20

8. Advanced Workflows: Batch Tracking and Traceability

For industries regulated by strict quality or safety standards (e.g., Food & Beverage, Pharmaceuticals, Electronics), the 'Make' module integrates with Batch Tracking (Lot Tracking).23

8.1. Batch Assignment Logic

Batch tracking is not automatic; it requires deliberate workflow steps.

  • Input Batches: If the ingredients used are batch-tracked, the user (or operator) must select which specific batch of raw material was consumed. This establishes the "chain of custody."
  • Output Batches: When an MO is completed, the user must assign a Batch Number to the finished goods. This can be a manually entered code (e.g., "LOT-2023-10-25") or generated via an internal naming convention.23
  • Constraint: An MO cannot be marked as "Done" if it produces a batch-tracked product but has no batch number assigned. This system constraint enforces compliance.23

8.2. Traceability and Recall Management

The data captured in the 'Make' module creates a bi-directional traceability map.

  • Forward Trace: "Which customers received products made from Batch X of raw material?" (Crucial if a supplier notifies you of a contaminated ingredient).
  • Backward Trace: "Which supplier provided the raw material for this defective unit sold to Customer Y?"
  • Expiry Dates: Users can also set expiration dates at the MO completion stage. This data flows into the inventory system, enabling First-Expired-First-Out (FEFO) picking logic in the Sales module, optimizing inventory freshness.23

9. Automation and Extensibility: The API Architecture

For modern enterprises, the 'Make' module does not exist in a vacuum. It interacts with external systems via the Katana API, allowing for custom automation and reporting.

9.1. API Data Structure

The Manufacturing Order object is accessible via the API, exposing a rich schema for developers.24

  • Endpoints: GET /v1/manufacturing_orders allows retrieval of the schedule. POST endpoints allow for the creation of MOs from external triggers (e.g., a custom e-commerce portal).
  • Structure: The API object includes nested arrays for ingredients and operations. This means an external system can not only read the status of an order but can also update the "Actual Quantity" or "Actual Time" programmatically.

9.2. Use Cases for Automation

  • Custom Dashboards: Companies can pull MO data to build large-screen visualizations for the factory floor, showing "Orders Due Today" or "Current Throughput" in real-time.
  • Automated Quality Blocking: An integration with a digital Quality Assurance (QA) system could automatically update an MO's status to "Blocked" via the API if a digital inspection fails.
  • Predictive Maintenance: Analyzing the "Actual Time" data from the API to identify machines (Resources) that are gradually slowing down, indicating a need for maintenance before a breakdown occurs.

10. Conclusion

The Katana 'Make' module represents a sophisticated synthesis of planning and execution. By moving away from static allocation to a dynamic, Rank-based waterfall logic, it empowers manufacturers to prioritize their most critical orders with agility. The seamless integration of the Schedule tab (for the planner) and the Tasks tab/Shop Floor App (for the operator) closes the feedback loop, ensuring that the strategic plan is executed accurately and that the physical reality of the floor is reflected in the digital ledger.

However, the system's power relies on operational discipline. The "Soft Allocation" model means that the "Ingredients Availability" traffic light is only as reliable as the priority list itself. A neglected Schedule tab with obsolete orders at Rank #1 will distort the availability data for every other order in the system. Therefore, the successful deployment of Katana’s 'Make' module requires not just user training on "which button to click," but a deeper understanding of the flow of priority, the importance of real-time status updates, and the financial implications of the data being generated. When wielded correctly, it transforms the manufacturing floor from a black box into a transparent, data-driven asset.
